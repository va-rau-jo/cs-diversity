Analysis

Disclaimer

Even just exploring the different reasons for why there is such a large racial and gender gap in computer science is already controversial. This is why there has been so much backlash against Stuart Reges, a computer science professor at the University of Washington, and James Damore, a former Google employee. Although I disagree with their points, rejecting and shaming their ideas should not be the default reaction.



Reges and Damore

In Reges’ article, he starts off by emphasizing that he is accepting of everyone in his classes and that he has advocated to get more women into computer science. Reges isn’t against diversity in computer science at all, although some interpretations of his article have claimed that. He is a stakeholder in this issue because teaching is his passion and he wants to see people succeed. Despite his past efforts and his very analytical explanations, several other sites have reported on his article and declared it to be misogynistic. I know several people who have read the secondhand sources but I highly recommend you read his original piece. In his article, he says that, “Women can code, but often they don’t want to. Why fight it? We will never reach gender parity. You can shame and fire all of the Damores you find, but that won’t change the underlying reality.” He comes to this conclusion and the only actual data presented is the growing disparity in women in computer science. He is not a woman in computer science, how could he possibly know this for certain?



Current Situation

Right now the generally agreed upon reason for this gap is that there is "systemic oppression" and a stigma that computer science is a male activity. While there is no “systemic oppression,” I do believe that most people think of computer science as an activity for nerdy guys and at times that culture can be overwhelming. Awareness of the problem is growing following the articles from these two, and there are many ways to protect yourself from discrimination. Major tech companies like Google and Microsoft are very inclusive and accepting of everyone and there are many universities throughout the United States, especially on the west coast, that have much higher male/female ratios in computer science (University of Washington, UC Berkeley, etc).



My Opinion

I personally have never seen oppression or unjust treatment to someone because of their race or gender in computer science. In fact, I think there is so much pressure on universities and companies to be more diverse that I think at times it is actually benefial to be a minority in computer science. I am not a woman though so I can’t offer my perspective on being a woman in computer science. But since this gap is such a prominent issue and people discuss it constantly, I am now much more aware of how things are outside of Washington State. If I end up working in computer science outside the United States or even outside Washington, I am sure to be affected. Morally, I feel obligated to spread awareness.
