Funk, Cary, and Kim Parker. “Racial Diversity and Discrimination in the U.S. STEM Workforce.” Pew Research Center's Social & Demographic Trends Project, Pew Research Center's Social & Demographic Trends Project, 9 Jan. 2018, www.pewsocialtrends.org/2018/01/09/blacks-in-stem-jobs-are-especially-concerned-about-diversity-and-discrimination-in-the- workplace/

This study conducted by the Pew Research Center provides extremely useful statistics on minority groups, especially African Americans, in computer science. Having statistics adds ethos to the argument that this is a real problem and they are hard to come by. This was also published in the last year, meaning the information is up to date and still relevant. The statistics range from peoples’ opinions on if diversity is given enough attention in the workplace, the percentages of workplace discrimination by race, and what percent of people believe their race has made job success harder. A page showing the reader statistics like these will provide the concrete data that is needed for a complete argument.

Reges, Stuart. “Why Women Don't Code.” Quillette, Quillette Pty Ltd , 19 June 2018, quillette.com/2018/06/19/why-women-dont-code/.

This article by University of Washington principal lecturer Stuart Reges offers a different point of view on gender discrimination in computer science. He tries to offer a scientific reason for why women do not pursue computer science and why they are generally uninterested in it. There was major backlash on this article because many people interpreted it as being misogynistic. However, Reges’ argument is based purely on statistics and he has already demonstrated his enthusiasm for accepting everyone into computer science and for getting more women into computer science. He mentions how more effort has been put into educating and accepting more women, yet the percentage of women entering computer science has increased less than the male population. This was published very recently and will be a good starting point for discussing the counterarguments against the systemic discrimination in computer science. Reges doesn’t discuss racial discrimination or encouragement of racial minorities to enter computer science, but the attitude towards gender and racial discrimination are almost identical.

Stoet, G. and Geary, D. (2018). The Gender-Equality Paradox in Science, Technology, Engineering, and Mathematics Education. 1st ed. [ebook] Leeds: Sage Journals, pp.4-9. Available at: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/pdf/10.1177/0956797617741719 [Accessed 21 Nov. 2018].

This research paper published in 2018 on the disparity between men and women in STEM fields attributes the disparity to women having less need for financial independence. Countries were compared by their Global Gender Gap Index score (1 being absolute parity) and the percentage of women in STEM fields and a strong correlation was found. The higher the country’s GGGI score, the fewer women it had in STEM fields. Countries like the United Arab Emirates and Algeria had a much higher percentage of women in STEM fields than Iceland and Norway. This provides another alternate viewpoint that claims that the lack of female minorities is due to their values and lifestyle, not discrimination. Addressing the counterarguments is going to be a major part of the website and disagreeing with this claim is going to be crucial.

Wakabayashi, Daisuke. “Former Uber Engineer's Lawsuit Claims Sexual Harassment.” The New York Times, The New York Times, 21 May 2018, www.nytimes.com/2018/05/21/technology/uber-sexual-harassment-lawsuit.html.

Workplace sexual harassment and discrimination seems to have become a common occurrence at Uber. This source is reputable because it was published in The New York Times and the author, Wakabayashi, is a consistent reporter on tech companies. This article talks about references Ingrid Avendano, a former Uber employee, and her ongoing lawsuit against Uber as well as the and a 420 person collective action lawsuit that Uber was settled for $10 million. Both of these lawsuits, both are regarding sexual harassment. In Wakabayashi’s article, he mentions that the common problems that people face when they identify discrimination and harassment against them is that they have to report people higher up in the company so their and their claims are sometimes disregarded. This makes several tech companies exclusionary to female and minority groups despite there not being any difference in policy regarding race or gender.
